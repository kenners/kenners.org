Stimulus depolarises membrane to theshold potential of ‚Äì55 mV 2. Voltage‚Äìgated Na+ channels open 3. Na+ membrane permeability suddenly increases 4. This generates a *larger* potential difference than the stimulus (‚Äì70 to +50 mV ‚âà 120mV) 5. Voltage‚Äìgated Na+ channels rapidly inactivate 6. Delayed action voltage‚Äìgated K+ channels open 7. Membrane potential becomes negative again as Na+ permeability falls and K+ permeability increases --- ![](membrane-perm.jpg) --- # Features of action potentials Propogation is 2-stage: 1. **Passive spread** of depolarisation to the adjacent area of membrane via local currents 2. **Regeneration** through the action potential as the resultant potential difference is amplified back to full size --- # Features of action potentials Both **positive** and **negative** feedback loops are involved: - "All or none" effect is due to the positive feedback loop between membrane depolarisation and Na+ permeability - Delayed action voltage‚Äìgated K+ channels produce negative feedback, returning membrane to its resting potential Only a small number of ions flow during channel opening ‚Äì the change in membrane permeability is the most significant effect on potential difference, not the number of ions flowing --- # Refractory period - Absolute refractory period: - Na+ channels are still open and cell is completely inexcitable - Relative refractory period: - 10‚Äì15 ms when a further action potential can only be triggered by greater than normal stimuli The refractory period ensures that the action potential can only spread in one direction. --- # Voltage‚Äìgated Na+ channel ![](na-channel-1.jpg) --- # Voltage‚Äìgated Na+ channel 4 domains of 6 Œ±‚Äìhelices, with three states: 1. **Phase 0:** Rapid depolarisation by the fast $i\_{Na}$ from Na+ channels transiently opening - **Phase 1:** Partial repolarisation ‚Äì a rapid decrease in Na+ permeability - **Phase 2:** Plateau ‚Äì slow Ca2+ channels open allowing inward flow of Ca2+, maintaining depolarisation and contraction - Catecholamines increase activation of L-type Ca2+ channels, whilst Ca2+ channel antagonists (e.g. verapamil) reduce it - **Phase 3:** Repolarisation ‚Äì Na+, K+, and Ca2+ permeabilities return to normal - **Phase 4:** Resting ‚Äì membrane potential governed mainly by potassium --- # Sinoatrial node No resting state: intrinsic pacemaker potential generates autorhythmicity Maximum polarisation of ‚Äì55 mV during diastole No Phase 1 or 2 as no plateau phase --- ![](san-ap.jpg) --- # Sinoatrial node - **Phase 4:** Spontaneous diastolic depolarisation shifts membrane to action potential threshold by $i\_f$ (slow influx of Ca2+) - **Phase 0:** Depolarisation by opening long-lasting voltage-gated Ca2+ channels and inward movement of Ca2+ ($i\_{CaL}$). - *No Na+ current involved at the SAN* - Low peak depolarisation of 20mV - **Phase 3:** Repolarisation: - Reduction in depolarising currents ($i\_f$ & $i\_{CaL}$ inactivated at positive potentials) - Increase in repolarising currents ($i\_K$ activated by positive potentials) causing late increase in K+ permeability $i\_f$ (funny current) is a high background leak of Ca2+ through Na+ channels. --- # Autonomic effects on SAN Parasympathetics increase K+ permeability in the SAN, hyperpolarising it and inhibiting spontaneous depolarisation.
